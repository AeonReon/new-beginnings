# Age 4 — Practical Reference

What a just-turned-four child is developmentally ready for and what the best homeschool / alternative-ed communities actually do with a 4-year-old day-to-day.

## 1. Developmental Milestones at Age 4

**Gross motor (AAP/CDC 2022 milestones):** Hops on one foot 2–3 times, catches a large ball most of the time, pours drinks with some spilling, unbuttons some buttons, walks up stairs alternating feet. Can pedal a tricycle confidently, many ready for a balance bike or small pedal bike. Climbs with confidence.

**Fine motor:** Holds a crayon with a "tripod-ish" grasp (true dynamic tripod often not until 5–6 — normal variation). Draws a person with 3+ parts. Cuts along a line with child scissors. Strings beads. Builds tower of 10 blocks. Threads laces. Some 4s still palmar-gripping is within normal range.

**Cognitive (Piaget's preoperational stage, 2–7):** Thinking is symbolic but pre-logical. Magical thinking dominant. Egocentrism (struggles to take another's perspective). Conservation not yet developed (taller glass has "more" water). Classifies by one attribute at a time. Counts meaningfully to ~10. Understands "same/different," basic time concepts (morning/night, not hours).

**Language:** 1,000–2,000+ word vocabulary. Sentences of 4–6+ words. Asks incessant "why" and "how." Tells simple stories. Understands prepositions (under, behind). Should be ~100% intelligible to strangers by 4 — persistent unintelligibility is a flag for SLP referral. Enjoys rhyme, nonsense, wordplay (strong predictor of phonological awareness).

**Social/emotional (Erikson: Initiative vs. Guilt, 3–6):** Developmental task is to initiate, plan, and carry out projects without being shamed. Parallel play shifts toward cooperative play. Imaginary friends and roleplay peak. Big feelings, still-developing regulation. Morality rule-based and absolute. Beginning of theory of mind.

**Montessori first plane (0–6):** "Absorbent mind" and "sensitive periods" — language, order, small objects, refinement of senses, social grace. At 4 the child is mid-plane: hands-on, sensorial, reality-based work is the developmental vehicle.

**Steiner/Waldorf (first seven-year cycle, 0–7):** Child doing the "work of incarnating" — building the physical body. Learning through imitation, rhythm, movement. Imagination flowers around 4. Formal academics held off until the change of teeth (~6–7).

**Normal variation to flag:** Handedness can shift until ~5. Letter reversals age-appropriate through 6–7. Some 4s read spontaneously; others won't for 2–3 years — both normal. Red flags (no pretend play, no back-and-forth conversation, loss of skills, no interest in other children) warrant evaluation.

## 2. Reading Readiness at 4 — The Tradition Debate

- **Montessori:** Sandpaper letters (phonetic sounds) from ~3. Pink/blue/green series. Many children read between 4 and 5½. Writing often precedes reading via the moveable alphabet.
- **Charlotte Mason:** Formal reading lessons often begin around 6. Before: being read to constantly, nursery rhymes, narration of picture books, outdoor observation.
- **Waldorf:** Deliberate delay — no formal reading before age 7 / first grade. Letters introduced through story and image. Oral culture first.
- **Classical:** *The Ordinary Parent's Guide to Teaching Reading* (Jessie Wise) — systematic synthetic phonics, "when child is ready" (often 4–5), 10–15 min lessons.
- **Synthetic phonics programs:** Jolly Phonics (multisensory, one sound per day), Teach Your Child to Read in 100 Easy Lessons (scripted). Many families do this at 4½–5. Reading Eggs (screen-based).

**The research (what actually predicts later reading):**
- **Oral language depth** — strongest predictor (Hart & Risley; Fernald).
- **Phonological awareness** — rhyme, alliteration, syllable clapping. Trainable through **play**, not worksheets.
- **Print awareness and letter-sound knowledge**.
- **Being read to daily** — *Becoming a Nation of Readers* (Anderson et al., 1985): "the single most important activity."
- **Background knowledge** — Hirsch's *Knowledge Gap* thesis.

**App guidance:** A 4-year-old does not need to read. A 4-year-old does need a rich oral/phonological/read-aloud diet. If readiness signals appear (asks what words say, plays with rhyme, traces letters spontaneously), short gentle phonics is fine. If not, wait.

## 3. Math / Numeracy at 4

**Developmentally appropriate:**
- **Subitising** — instantly seeing "3" without counting (dot cards, dice games). Stronger predictor of later math than rote counting.
- **One-to-one correspondence** — touching each object as you count.
- **Rote counting** to 20–30, errors expected in the teens.
- **Cardinality** — last number said = the total.
- **Comparison** — more/less/same.
- **Pattern** — AB, ABB, ABC with objects.
- **Shape** and **spatial language** (over, between, through).

**Materials:**
- Montessori number rods, spindle boxes, sandpaper numerals, golden beads (4½+).
- **Cuisenaire rods** — 4+, relational/structural thinking. Gattegno's approach; Education Unboxed free videos.
- **RightStart Math Level A** — abacus, 4–5.
- **Math with Confidence (Kate Snow) Kindergarten** — gentle, developmentally sound.
- Cooking, setting the table, counting steps — practical numeracy beats worksheets.

**Premature:** Timed drills, worksheets, memorising math facts before conceptual grounding, abstract symbols before many concrete experiences.

## 4. Handwriting / Pre-Writing at 4

At 4 the goal is **hand strength and control**, not pretty letters.

**Pre-writing hierarchy:** vertical line → horizontal → circle → cross → square → diagonal → X → triangle. Most 4-year-olds have the first 4–5; triangle often 4½–5.

**Strengthening activities:** Playdough, clay, tongs, tweezers, clothes pegs, threading, hole-punching, tearing paper, wringing sponges, spray bottles, chalk on vertical surfaces, cutting. Practical life work *is* handwriting prep.

**Programs:** **Handwriting Without Tears** — "Get Set for School" pre-K uses wood pieces, chalkboard, "Mat Man" song. Waldorf uses beeswax and form drawing. Montessori sandpaper letters (muscle memory) before pencil work.

**Avoid:** Pushing pencil-and-paper letter formation before tripod grasp matures. Can cement bad grips.

## 5. Daily Rhythms — Real Homeschool Examples

### A. Charlotte Mason "Year 0" (pre-formal, 4–5), Ambleside Online style
- 7:30 Breakfast, dressed, chores
- 8:30 Morning Basket: poem, hymn, folk song, short Bible, one picture book
- 9:00 Outdoor play (4–6 hrs if weather allows)
- 12:30 Lunch, read-aloud
- 1:30 Rest / quiet time / audiobook in bed
- 3:00 Free play, handicraft, helping with dinner
- 6:30 Family read-aloud, bath, bed
- **No formal lessons.**

### B. Wild+Free rhythm
- Slow morning, unhurried breakfast
- Morning basket (poetry tea, nature poem, picture book stack)
- Long outdoor/nature block — often 2–3 hrs, any weather
- Lunch + read-aloud (living book)
- Quiet hour (independent play, audiobooks)
- Afternoon: open-ended — art invitation, baking, free play
- Heavy sibling-together, mixed ages, nature.

### C. Montessori-at-home
- 7:30 Breakfast child helps prepare
- 8:30–11:30 **3-hour work cycle**: child chooses from prepared shelves (5–8 trays)
- Adult observes, redirects gently
- Outdoor time mid-cycle if child needs movement
- 12:00 Lunch (child sets/clears)
- Rest
- Afternoon: outdoor, library, errands

### D. Waldorf home rhythm
- Strong **weekly rhythm** — each weekday has a colour and activity
- Morning: circle time with verse and song, handwork, outside
- Wet-on-wet watercolour or beeswax modelling weekly
- Seasonal nature table kept and updated
- Mid-afternoon: same fairy tale told (not read) across a week
- Early dinner, early bed. **No screens.**

### E. Peaceful Press Playful Pioneers-adjacent
- Morning basket with devotional, poem, picture book
- One short skill (letter of the week, counting game) — 10 min
- Nature walk
- Free play / invitation to play
- Read-aloud, rest

### F. Classical / Well-Trained Mind-lite
- 10 min phonics (OPGTR) *only if child is ready*
- 10 min counting/pattern game
- 20 min read-aloud (picture books, poetry, Aesop)
- Outside, free play, nap
- Evening: family read-aloud chapter book

**Common anchors:** morning ritual, outdoor time, read-aloud, rest/quiet, free play, shared meals, early bedtime. Differences are more aesthetic than structural at 4.

## 6. Free Play — The Research

**Peter Gray** (*Free to Learn*, Boston College): free play — child-directed, mixed-age, lightly supervised, with real risk — builds executive function, resilience, intrinsic motivation. Longitudinal argument: decline in free play since the 1950s correlates with rising anxiety, depression, decreased locus of control.

**Lenore Skenazy / Let Grow:** *Free-Range Kids* (2009). Practical toolkit for restoring independence.

**How much at 4?** AAP 2018 clinical report ("The Power of Play"): play is essential, not a break from real learning. Heuristic: **several hours of child-directed play daily, with at least some outdoors**.

**App framing:** Track free play as a core metric, not a gap.

## 7. Read-Alouds

**Key voices:**
- **Jim Trelease, *The Read-Aloud Handbook*** — cites *Becoming a Nation of Readers*.
- **Sarah Mackenzie, *The Read-Aloud Family* / Read-Aloud Revival podcast**.
- **Gladys Hunt, *Honey for a Child's Heart*** — annotated booklists.
- **Ambleside Online Year 0** — Beatrix Potter, Mother Goose, Blueberries for Sal, Make Way for Ducklings, Milly-Molly-Mandy, Burgess Bird Book, Wind in the Willows.
- **Five in a Row** — one picture book per week.
- **Sonlight P4/5** booklist.

**"Living book":** single author with passion, narrative voice, literary quality, respects the child's intelligence. Opposite of twaddle.

## 8. Other Essentials

**Nature study:** Weekly nature walk with a small journal. Anna Botsford Comstock's *Handbook of Nature Study* (1911, free online). At 4: notice, name, draw (or have parent scribe).

**Picture study:** One artist per term (12 weeks), 6 paintings. Look quietly 2–3 min, turn over, narrate.

**Composer study:** One composer per term. **Classical Kids** audio series widely loved.

**Handcraft:** Finger knitting, weaving, sewing cards, beeswax. Pre-handcraft hand-strengthening.

**Practical life / real chores:** Child sets table, pours own water, folds napkins, sweeps, waters plants, feeds pet, helps cook. Real tools.

**Outdoor time:** Scandinavian *friluftsliv* — **3+ hours outside daily**, all weather. Linda McGurk's *There's No Such Thing as Bad Weather*. Louv, *Last Child in the Woods*.

## 9. What NOT to Do at 4

- **Formal seated desk work for extended blocks.**
- **Tests and grades.**
- **Heavy memorisation drill.**
- **"Educational" screen apps as primary learning mode.** AAP: <1 hr/day co-viewed high-quality content for 2–5s.
- **Over-scheduling** — more than one enrichment class crowds out free play.
- **Pushing reading/writing before readiness signals.**

**Evidence base:**
- Hirsh-Pasek & Golinkoff, *Einstein Never Used Flashcards* (2003), *A Mandate for Playful Learning in Preschool* (2009).
- Alliance for Childhood, *Crisis in the Kindergarten* (2009).
- Rebecca Marcon (2002) — academically focused preschoolers showed lower grades by 4th grade.
- Finnish/Nordic evidence — formal schooling at 7; Finland strong on PISA. Pasi Sahlberg, *Finnish Lessons*.
- Sebastian Suggate (2009, 2013) — no long-term advantage for early readers by age 10–11.

## 10. Progress Monitoring Without Schoolification

**Portfolio approach (primary):**
- **Photo diary** — 1–2 photos/week of child at work/play.
- **Work samples** — dated drawings, first written letters, nature journal pages, in a folder per year.
- **Parent narration log** — 2–3 sentences, once or twice/week.
- **Audio** — record child narrating a book, singing a song, retelling a story.
- **Nature journal** (child's) — even scribbles dated are a record.

**Observation checklists (not tests):** Frame as "have I seen this?" with a date:
- Gross motor, fine motor, language, phonological awareness, numeracy, social, practical life, concentration.

**What to avoid surfacing:** anything that looks like a gradebook, rank, percentile, "falling behind" at 4. Frame as observation and celebration. If a checklist item is missing by 5, surface gently as "something to introduce" — not "a gap."
