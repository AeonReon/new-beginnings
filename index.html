New Beginnings

A first-principles guide for home-educating a four-year-old. Not a curriculum. Not a scorecard. A companion.

What this is

A single, unhurried year laid out for the parent of a child who has just turned four. Six sections — Compass, Compare, Rhythm, Feast, Resources, Keeping — each answering a different question a parent asks in the first months of home education.

The app will not tell you what to do. It will tell you what other people who have thought carefully about this do do, and why. You decide.

The first principle

The parent using this app has already refused something — the modern school model of sitting still, being ranked, and memorising what someone else decided was important. The question is what to do instead.

The answer this app defends is not a tradition, religious or secular, taken on authority. It is this: every practice has to have a reason you can state plainly. "Because Charlotte Mason said so" is not a reason. "Because the Bible says so" is not a reason. "Because schools do this" is not a reason either.

Get to the root of why we're doing things rather than dogmatic unknown and unprovable things. Which happens in religious and non-religious education.

So every practice in this app is followed by a short why — the actual developmental, philosophical, or observational reason the practice works. If a reason can't be given, the practice doesn't make it in.

The core commitments

1. Character before content

A 4-year-old who can pay attention, tell the truth, and do hard things will learn to read when the time comes. A 4-year-old who can read at four but cannot attend or tell the truth has been badly served.

Habits laid down at four run the engine for life. Reading laid down at four gives a two-year head start that is neutralised by age ten in every longitudinal study that has looked. The virtues compound; the skills don't.

2. Free play is the curriculum at four

Hours outdoors, siblings or friends in the mud, blocks on a rug, dolls given voices — this is not a break from learning. This is the learning.

A 4-year-old's nervous system is still being built. It is built by motion, risk, mess, and the thousand small decisions of self-directed play. Replacing play with "educational" activity at four is a trade that costs more than it pays.

3. Rooted in tradition, tested by reason

The Greek, Roman, and Christian traditions thought long and hard about what it means to form a human being. A lot of what they concluded is still right. Some isn't. Draw on what works; name what doesn't.

A tradition is an accumulation of tested trial and error over many generations. Throwing it out costs you everything it knows. Taking it on without thinking costs you the freedom to correct its errors. Both refuse what the tradition itself requires: reasoning.

4. Beauty is not optional

A poem well-chosen, a piece of music worth hearing, a painting looked at quietly — these are not enrichment. They are the actual work of shaping what the child loves.

Children become what they repeatedly attend to. The inner furniture of a 4-year-old who hears Mozart is different from the inner furniture of a 4-year-old who hears pop jingles. Neither is neutral. Choose.

5. Real work over pretend work

Chopping banana with a butter knife, sweeping a floor, folding napkins, polishing leaves. A 4-year-old wants to contribute. Let them.

Competence comes from doing things that actually matter. Toy kitchens teach the child that their work is not real. Real kitchens teach the child that they are a person among persons.

6. Slow, unhurried, repeatable

The same verse memorised across four weeks. The same story told for a fortnight. The same tree visited each season. Repetition is not a failure of imagination; it is how depth is made.

A 4-year-old asks for the same story seven times because on the seventh telling they understand something new. A child who consumes novelty never reaches depth in anything. The parent's job is to slow down, not speed up.

The shape of a year

The Feast section lays out 52 weeks. Each week has one poem, one hymn or folk song, one composer piece, one painting, one story, one nature prompt, and one practical-life activity. The content is already chosen — you don't have to plan, you just have to open it and do it.

You won't do all of it every week. You'll do some of it. That is the point.

What you can ignore

Things the app does not do

  • No grades. There is no percentile, no rank, no "behind."
  • No child-facing screens. This app is for you. The child never opens it.
  • No AI chat. The content is curated so the voice stays coherent.
  • No social feed. If you want community, the Resources page links to the real ones (Ambleside, Wild + Free, Scholé Sisters).
  • No cloud account. Everything is in your browser. Clearing site data wipes your logs — you can also export them to JSON from Keeping.

Parent reading list

If the app's voice is agreeable, these are the sources. Read slowly; one at a time:

  • Charlotte Mason, Home Education (Vol. 1, 1886) — free at amblesideonline.org. Dense Victorian prose. Skim and return.
  • Anthony Esolen, Ten Ways to Destroy the Imagination of Your Child — short, sharp, a diagnostic list of what modern childhood has lost.
  • Vigen Guroian, Tending the Heart of Virtue — on why fairy tales, unsanitised, form moral imagination.
  • Stratford Caldecott, Beauty for Truth's Sake — the classical liberal arts rehabilitated for a parent.
  • Peter Gray, Free to Learn — the research on why play matters and what happens without it.
  • Kim John Payne, Simplicity Parenting — on slowing down and saying no to busyness.
  • Angela Hanscom, Balanced and Barefoot — OT-grounded case for outdoor, messy, physical childhood.
  • Susan Wise Bauer, The Well-Trained Mind — classical home education, end-to-end. Use for perspective.

Don't feel obliged to finish any of them. A book the parent reads thinking is worth more than a book read through.
