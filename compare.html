What others do at four

Six traditions that thoughtful parents follow. Each is summarised as its practitioners would recognise it. Strengths and limits are listed. At the end: what this app borrows from each, and why.

What this app borrows

From Charlotte Mason (most)

Living books, narration, picture and composer study, nature journals, habit training, short lessons, and the reading list.

Mason was already a classical humanist. Her method honours the developmental reality of a 4yo while feeding them the real tradition of the West from the start.

From Waldorf (rhythm + aesthetic, not metaphysics)

Predictable daily shape, weekly anchors (baking day, painting day), seasonal nature table, festival life, natural materials, stories told and re-told.

A 4yo's nervous system settles under predictable rhythm. Natural materials give richer sensory feedback. The underlying anthroposophy is optional; the practices stand on their own.

From Wild + Free (outdoors, slowness)

Hours outside every day, in all weathers. Unhurried mornings. Childhood as protected.

The research on outdoor time for attention, mood, sleep, and immunity is strong. Slowness is not a luxury; it is a condition of depth.

From Montessori (practical life + early math)

Real work with real tools; child-sized brooms, pitchers, knives. Sandpaper letters and numerals. Number rods, golden beads.

Practical life builds competence and concentration. The math materials make abstract ideas touchable, which is exactly what a 4yo needs.

From the classical tradition (frame)

Paideia as the goal — the formation of a whole person, oriented toward truth, goodness, beauty. Memory as filling the well. Virtue as habit. Stories as training of moral imagination.

Twenty-five centuries of careful thinking about how humans are formed. Much of it is still right. None of it is beyond questioning.

What the app does not take

  • Montessori's prohibition on fantasy play — fairy tales do real work on moral imagination.
  • Waldorf's insistence on delayed reading when a child is ready earlier.
  • Sayers-style "grammar stage" fact-memorisation — a 4yo is not ready and may learn to hate it.
  • Classical Conversations' co-op academic pressure at four.
  • Any claim that a single method has it all right.
