Keeping

A way to remember what this year looked like. Not a scorecard. Narration log, habit tracker, a simple diary, an observation list. Everything stays on your device. Export to JSON any time.

Why "keeping" and not "tracking"

Charlotte Mason used the verb keep for journals — a nature journal was a "nature keep," a book of quotations a "commonplace book." The word is right. You are not measuring; you are keeping faith with what happened.

The difference matters. Measurement asks: did we hit the target? Keeping asks: what do I want to remember? The first creates anxiety. The second creates an archive.

Narration log

After a read-aloud, ask the child: "Tell me what happened." Write down what they say, as near verbatim as you can. One sentence at four is plenty.

Narration is the oldest form of active recall. The child who can tell a story back has understood it; the child who cannot has not. Over months you see their narrations grow from single images ("the fox couldn't reach") to whole arcs.

Habit tracker

One habit at a time. Four to six weeks. Mason's rule: lay the habit down as a rail so the child's life can run on it without friction.

Trying to install five habits at once installs none. The brain needs repetition of one clear thing. Once it's automatic, start the next.

Diary

A sentence or two, most days. What happened. What the child said. What you noticed. No format required. No photo required — describe in words if you like.

A year of one-sentence entries becomes, in retrospect, a book. Waiting for the "important" moments gives you a blank year. Write the small things.

Observations

"Have I seen this?" — not "can the child pass a test?" Tick things off as you notice them naturally. Don't set up a test. Don't worry about missing ones.

This is not a milestone checklist. It is a way of training your own attention toward what a 4-year-old is actually doing. Each item is what it looks like when a 4yo is developing typically; all normal children hit these at different times.

If something is missing at five

A missed item at age 5 is a cue to introduce the underlying activity, not a cause for worry. If a handful are missing and your gut says something is off, speak to a paediatrician or relevant specialist (GP, SLP, OT). Most things that look delayed at four catch up.
